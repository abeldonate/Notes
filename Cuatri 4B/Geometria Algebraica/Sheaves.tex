\section{Sheaves}

The introduction of the concept of sheaf is essential for the study of schemes. Sheaves are a general tool to study topological spaces through the study of a collection of associated objects in a fixed category. The way those objects are "glued" (we will define properly what this concept means) determines, in some fashion, how the topological space behaves.

Informally, a sheaf encodes the information of the topological space by associating a object of the category (set, group, ring, module, etc) to each open set of the topology. However, this mapping is not arbitrary, it must accomplish some "restriction" property, that is, the mapping of a subset must behave well with the mapping of the set.

Now we have gained intuition about what is a sheaf, we define it properly

\begin{definition}[Presheaf and sheaf] A presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of abelian groups associated to a topological space $X$ consists of the data
\begin{enumerate}
    \item for each open set $U\subset X$, an abelian group $\mathcal{F}(U)$
    \item for each inclusion of open sets $V\hookrightarrow U$ a restriction map $\rho_{UV} : \mathcal{F}(U)\to \mathcal{F}(V)$ such that $\rho_{UU}= \id_{\mathcal{F}(U)}$ and $\rho _{VW}\circ \rho _{UV} = \rho _{UW}$ for the inclusion maps $W\hookrightarrow V \hookrightarrow U$
\end{enumerate}

In addition, a presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ is a sheaf if satisfies the following conditions
\begin{enumerate}
    \item (Gluing) If $\{V_i\}$ is an open covering of the open set $U$, and $s_i\in \mathcal{F}(V_i)$ satisfy $\rho_{V_i, V_i\cap V_j} s_i = \rho_{V_j, V_i\cap V_j} s_j$, then there exists a lifting $s\in \mathcal{F}(U)$ such that $\rho_{U, V_i} s = s_i\ \forall i$
    \item (Locality) If $s, t \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ and $\rho _{U, V_i}s = \rho_{U, V_i}t\ \forall i$, then $s = t$ 
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The definition of sheaf for other categories is analogous by changing abelian groups with rings, sets, modules, etc. The elements of a sheaf $s\in \mathcal{F}(U)$ are called sections.  



The two requirements for a sheaf are extremely important. The gluing axiom tells us that if the sheaves behaves well in the intersections via the restriction maps, then they lead to a larger sheaf. The locality axiom states that sections are uniquely determined by their restrictions.  

We provide some examples of sheaves and presheaves.

\begin{example} Let $M$ be a $\mathcal{C}^k-$manifold. Then, $\mathcal{F} := \mathcal{C}^k(-)$, such that $\mathcal{C}^k(-)$ is the map associating each open set $U$ to the ring of $k-$differentiable functions. The restriction maps are obviously defined by restricting the domain of the functions. This is valid also for smooth manifolds. 
\end{example}

\begin{example} Let $\mathcal{C}_b(U)$ be the continuous bounded functions on an open set $U \in \mathcal{R}$. Then the mapping $\mathcal{F}:= \mathcal{C}_b(-)$ is a presheaf, but not a sheaf over the real line.
\end{example}

\begin{proof}
    This can be seen by considering the open covering of the real line whose open sets are $U_n = (n-1, n+1) \ \forall n\in \mathbb{Z}$. If we consider the presheaf $\mathcal{C}_b(-)$, then it clearly satisfies the locality axiom, but not the gluing axiom, since the section $s\in \mathcal{F}$ defined by $s: \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R} , x \to x$, is not a bounded function globally altough it is locally (in every $U_i$).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Stalk] Let $\mathcal{F} $ a presheaf and $P \in X$. The stalk of $\mathcal{F} $ at $P$ is defined as the direct limit of the groups
    \[
    \mathcal{F}_P = \varinjlim_{P \in U} \mathcal{F} (U)
    \]
\end{definition}

The elements of the stalk can be understood in less abstract way by thinking about the equivalence classes of the pairs $<s, U> = (s,U)/ \sim$ with $s \in F(U), U \subseteq X$ that meet
\[
<s, U> = <t, V>  ( \iff  (s, U) \sim (t,V)) \iff  \ \exists W \subseteq U \cap V : s|_U = t|_V
\]

\begin{proposition} Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}  $ a morphism of sheaves. Then there exist an induced morphism in the stalks $\varphi _P: \mathcal{F}_P \to \mathcal{G}_P  $ for every point $P \in X$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The most natural induced morphism is the one that sends $ <s, U> \mapsto <\varphi (s),U> $. Obviously $<s, U> \in \mathcal{F}(U), <\varphi (s), U> \in \mathcal{F}(U)  $, so the only thing to prove is that this map is well defined. Let $<s, U> = <t, V> \in  \mathcal{F}_P $. Then there exist, by the construction of stalk, $W \subseteq U \cap V$ such that $s|_W = t|_W$, and thus $\varphi (s)|_W = \varphi (t)|_W$, getting
\[
<\varphi (s), U> = <\varphi (s)|_W, W> = <\varphi (t)|_W, W> = <\varphi (t), V>
\]
which proves that $\varphi _P(<s, U>) = \varphi _P(<t, V>)$ 
\end{proof}

Stalks are extremely useful in the study of sheaves. They can be able to generate global data from local one. The most extreme case is, for example, when $\mathcal{F}(U) $ is the ring of polynomials or the ring of analytic functions in the example avobe. I such cases one can recover the whole information of the sheaf $s \in \mathcal{F}(U) $ given the stalk $s_P \in \mathcal{F}_P $ on any point.

In general, this is not the case, but, fortunately, the knowledge of all the stalks can be glued to obtain the behaviour of the section of the sheave. The following proposition encloses this idea. 

\begin{proposition}
Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$. Then
\[
\varphi \text{ is an isomorphism } \iff \varphi _P \text{ is an isomorphism } \ \forall  P \in X
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First we prove $\boxed{ \implies } $. For the surjectivity of $\varphi _P$ suppose $<t, U> \in \mathcal{G}_P $. Then there exists $s \coloneq \varphi ^{-1}(t)$ and $\varphi _P( <s, U>) = \mathcal{G}_P $. For the injectivity we suppose $<\varphi (s), U> = <\varphi (t), V> = <\varphi (s|_W), W>$ with $W \subseteq U \cap V$. Then, by injectivity of $\varphi $ we have $\varphi (s|_W)=\varphi (t|_W) \implies  s|_W = t|_W \implies <s, U> = <t,V>$. 

The other direction $\boxed{ \impliedby } $  is a little bit more subtle. First we prove injectivity. Let $s \in \mathcal{F} (U) : \varphi (s)=0$. Then
\[
\varphi _P(<s, U>) = <\varphi (s), U> = <0, U> \ \forall  P \implies <s, U> = <0, U> \ \forall  P
\]
Now we know that we can restrict for all $P$ our domain $U$ in a smaller one $W_P \subseteq U$ in which $s|_{W_P} = 0$. Taking the covering $\{W_P\}_P$ of $U$ we get, by gluing property, $s = 0$. This proves the injectivity. 

For the surjectivity let $t \in \mathcal{G}(U) $. Then, for $<t, U> \in \mathcal{G}_P \ \exists <s^P, V> \in \mathcal{F}_P $ (we can choose a section that a priori depends on the point $P$) such that $\varphi _P(<s^P, V>) = <\varphi (s^P), V> = <t, U>$. By the definition of stalk we can find a neighborhood of $P$ such that $W_P \subseteq U \cap V$ such that $\varphi (s^P)|_{W_P} = t|_{W_P}$. We now have to see that the sections that induce the stalks can be glued in a global section, but this is consequence of the injectivity of $\varphi$, since $\varphi (s^P)|_{W_P \cap W_Q} = \varphi (s^Q)_{W_P \cap W_{Q}}$ implies $s^P|_{W_P \cap W_Q} = s^Q|_{W_P \cap W_Q}$. Thus, applying the gluing axiom we reconstruct a global section $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$. This proves surjectivity.
\end{proof}

Note that the given proof also shows that $\varphi $ injective (resp. surjective) $ \implies $ $\varphi _P$ injective (resp. surjective).
