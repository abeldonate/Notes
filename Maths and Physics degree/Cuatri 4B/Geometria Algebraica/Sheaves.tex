\section{Introduction}

This report is presented as the final project of the degree subject of Algebraic Geometry at Polytechnic University of Catalonia. The text aims to be a comprehensible introduction to sheaves, a compendium of solved exercises from \cite{Har}, and a very brief introduction to schemes, following \cite{eis}.

The introduction of the concept of sheaf is essential for the study of schemes. Sheaves are a general tool to study topological spaces through the study of a collection of associated objects in a fixed category. The way those objects are "glued" (we will define properly what this concept means) determines, in some fashion, how the topological space behaves.

\section{Sheaves}

Informally, a sheaf encodes the information of the topological space by associating an object of a fixed category (set, group, ring, module, etc) to each open set of the topology. However, this mapping is not arbitrary, it must accomplish some \textit{restriction} property, that is, the mapping of a subset must behave well with the mapping of the set.

Now we have gained intuition about what is a sheaf, we define it properly.

\begin{definition}[Presheaf and sheaf] A presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of abelian groups associated to a topological space $X$ consists of the data
\begin{enumerate}
    \item for each open set $U\subset X$, an abelian group $\mathcal{F}(U)$
    \item for each inclusion of open sets $V\hookrightarrow U$ a restriction map $\rho_{UV} : \mathcal{F}(U)\to \mathcal{F}(V)$ such that $\rho_{UU}= \id_{\mathcal{F}(U)}$ and $\rho _{VW}\circ \rho _{UV} = \rho _{UW}$ for the inclusion maps $W\hookrightarrow V \hookrightarrow U$
\end{enumerate}

In addition, a presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ is a sheaf if satisfies the following conditions on every covering $\{V_i\}$ of an open set $U$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item (Gluing) If $s_i\in \mathcal{F}(V_i)$ satisfy $\rho_{V_i, V_i\cap V_j} s_i = \rho_{V_j, V_i\cap V_j} s_j$, then there exists a lifting $s\in \mathcal{F}(U)$ such that $\rho_{U, V_i} s = s_i\ \forall i$
    \item (Locality) If $s, t \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ and $\rho _{U, V_i}s = \rho_{U, V_i}t\ \forall i$, then $s = t$ 
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The definition of sheaf for other categories is analogous and can be constructed by changing abelian groups with rings, sets, modules, etc. The elements of the abelian groups $s\in \mathcal{F}(U)$ are called sections.



The two requirements for a sheaf are extremely important. The glueing axiom tells us that if the sheaves behave well in the intersections via the restriction maps, then they lead to a larger sheaf. The locality axiom states that sections are uniquely determined by their restrictions.  

We provide some examples of sheaves and presheaves.

\begin{example} Let $M$ be a $\mathcal{C}^k-$ manifold. Then, $\mathcal{F} := \mathcal{C}^k(-)$, such that $\mathcal{C}^k(-)$ is the map associating each open set $U$ to the ring of $k-$differentiable functions. The restriction maps are obviously defined by restricting the domain of the functions. This is valid also for smooth manifolds. 
\end{example}

\begin{example} Let $\mathcal{C}_b(U)$ be the abelian group of  continuous bounded functions on an open set $U \in \mathcal{R}$. Then the mapping $\mathcal{F}:= \mathcal{C}_b(-)$ is a presheaf, but not a sheaf over the real line.
\end{example}

\begin{proof}
    This can be seen by considering the open covering of the real line whose open sets are $U_n = (n-1, n+1) \ \forall n\in \mathbb{Z}$. If we consider the presheaf $\mathcal{C}_b(-)$, then it clearly satisfies the locality axiom, but not the gluing axiom, since the section $s\in \mathcal{F}$ defined by $s: \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R} , x \to x$, is not a bounded function globally although it is locally (in every $U_i$).
\end{proof}

We now focus on a more localized study of our topological space. Fixing a point in the topological space $P \in X$, we can consider neighbourhoods smaller and smaller which induce abelian groups through the defined sheaf. This notion can be formally described in the following definition.

\begin{definition}[Stalk] Let $\mathcal{F} $ a presheaf and $P \in X$. The stalk of $\mathcal{F} $ at $P$ is defined as the direct limit of the groups
    \[
    \mathcal{F}_P = \varinjlim_{P \in U} \mathcal{F} (U)
    \]
\end{definition}

The elements of the stalk can be understood in a less abstract way by thinking about the equivalence classes of the pairs $<s, U> = (s,U)/ \sim$ with $s \in F(U), U \subseteq X$ that meet
\[
<s, U> = <t, V>  ( \iff  (s, U) \sim (t,V)) \iff  \ \exists W \subseteq U \cap V : s|_W = t|_W
\]

Now that we have morphisms between sheaves, classical objects such as kernel, image or cokernel can be defined in this category. 

\begin{definition}[Kernel and image]
Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ be a morphism of sheaves. We define:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item The kernel of $\varphi$ as the sheaf $\ker(\varphi )(U) = \ker (\varphi|_U)$. 
    \item The image of $\varphi $ as the sheafification of the presheaf $ \im(\varphi )(U) = \im(\varphi |_U)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}



\begin{proposition} Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}  $ a morphism of sheaves. Then there exists an induced morphism in the stalks $\varphi _P: \mathcal{F}_P \to \mathcal{G}_P  $ for every point $P \in X$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The most natural induced morphism is the one that sends $ <s, U> \mapsto <\varphi (s),U> $. Obviously $<s, U> \in \mathcal{F}(U), <\varphi (s), U> \in \mathcal{G}(U)  $, so the only thing to prove is that this map is well defined. Let $<s, U> = <t, V> \in  \mathcal{F}_P $. Then there exist, by the construction of stalk, $W \subseteq U \cap V$ such that $s|_W = t|_W$, and thus $\varphi (s)|_W = \varphi (t)|_W$, getting
\[
<\varphi (s), U> = <\varphi (s)|_W, W> = <\varphi (t)|_W, W> = <\varphi (t), V>
\]
which proves that $\varphi _P(<s, U>) = \varphi _P(<t, V>)$ 
\end{proof}

We provide some interesting examples of stalks of different sheaves on $X = \mathbb{R}$. 

\begin{example}
Let $X = \mathbb{R}$. We consider the following sheaves and compute the stalk in a point $P$:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item $\mathcal{F}(U) = \{f : f \in \mathbb{R}[x]\}$. Then $\mathcal{F}_P \cong \mathbb{R}[x]$ 
    \item $\mathcal{F}(U) = \{f : f \in \mathcal{C}^\omega \}$. Then $\mathcal{F}_P \cong k[[x]] $
\end{enumerate}
\end{example}

In the first example we observe that the sheaf is the constant sheaf, since it is not important the choice of the open set $U$. Thus, the stalk, thought as a direct limit of a constant ring, is simply the ring. In the second example we see that if $U \subseteq V$, then there are certain functions in $U$ that cannot be defined in $V$ (for example, the functions that explode in the boundary of $U$). The direct limit is the ring of formal series, since it suffices to know the function in a neighborhood to know how it behaves in an open set $U$ small enough.

Stalks are extremely useful in the study of sheaves. They can be able to generate global data from local one. The most extreme case is, for example, when $\mathcal{F}(U) $ is the ring of polynomials or the ring of analytic functions in the example above. In such cases one can recover the whole information of the sheaf $s \in \mathcal{F}(U) $ given the stalk $s_P \in \mathcal{F}_P $ on a single point.

In general, this is not the case, but, fortunately, the knowledge of all the stalks can be glued to obtain the behaviour of the section of the sheave. The following proposition encloses this idea. 


\begin{proposition}
Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$. Then
\[
\varphi \text{ (injective, surjective, iso) } \iff \varphi _P \text{ (injective, surjective, iso) } \ \forall  P \in X
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First we prove $\boxed{ \implies } $. For the surjectivity of $\varphi _P$ suppose $<t, U> \in \mathcal{G}_P $. Then there exists $s \coloneq \varphi ^{-1}(t)$ and $\varphi _P( <s, U>) = \mathcal{G}_P $. For the injectivity we suppose $<\varphi (s), U> = <\varphi (t), V> = <\varphi (s|_W), W>$ with $W \subseteq U \cap V$. Then, by injectivity of $\varphi $ we have $\varphi (s|_W)=\varphi (t|_W) \implies  s|_W = t|_W \implies <s, U> = <t,V>$. 

The other direction $\boxed{ \impliedby } $  is a little bit more subtle. First we prove injectivity. Let $s \in \mathcal{F} (U) : \varphi (s)=0$. Then
\[
\varphi _P(<s, U>) = <\varphi (s), U> = <0, U> \ \forall  P \implies <s, U> = <0, U> \ \forall  P
\]
Now we know that we can restrict for all $P$ our domain $U$ in a smaller one $W_P \subseteq U$ in which $s|_{W_P} = 0$. Taking the covering $\{W_P\}_P$ of $U$ we get, by gluing property, $s = 0$. This proves the injectivity. 

For the surjectivity let $t \in \mathcal{G}(U) $. Then, for $<t, U> \in \mathcal{G}_P \ \exists <s^P, V> \in \mathcal{F}_P $ (we can choose a section that a priori depends on the point $P$) such that $\varphi _P(<s^P, V>) = <\varphi (s^P), V> = <t, U>$. By the definition of stalk we can find a neighbourhood of $P$ such that $W_P \subseteq U \cap V$ such that $\varphi (s^P)|_{W_P} = t|_{W_P}$. We now have to see that the sections that induce the stalks can be glued in a global section, but this is consequence of the injectivity of $\varphi$, since $\varphi (s^P)|_{W_P \cap W_Q} = \varphi (s^Q)_{W_P \cap W_{Q}}$ implies $s^P|_{W_P \cap W_Q} = s^Q|_{W_P \cap W_Q}$. Thus, applying the gluing axiom we reconstruct a global section $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$. This proves surjectivity.
\end{proof}

We see that injectivity and surjectivity behave well when considering all the points in the stalk. This is not the case when checking surjectivity on the morphisms of abelian groups on open sets. In this case surjectivity fails. 

\begin{proposition} Let $\varphi :\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}  $. Then
    \[
    \varphi \text{ is injective } \iff \varphi _U : \mathcal{F} (U) \to \mathcal{G} \text{ is injective } \ \forall  U \subseteq X
    \]
    \[
    \varphi \text{ is surjective } \rlap{\(\quad\not\)}\implies \varphi _U : \mathcal{F} (U) \to \mathcal{G} \text{ is surjective } \ \forall  U \subseteq X
    \]
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Problems}

\begin{problem}
\textbf{1}. Let $A$ be an abelian group, and define the \textit{constant presheaf} associated to $A$ on the topological space $X$ to be the presheaf $U \to A$ for all $U \neq  \emptyset $, with restriction maps the identity. Show that the constant sheaf $\mathcal{A} $ defined in the text is the sheaf associated to this presheaf.   
\end{problem}
\begin{proof}
Let $\mathcal{F} $ the presheaf of the problem, $\mathcal{G}$ any sheaf and $\mathcal{A}$ the constant sheaf. Then suppose $ \varphi \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ is any morphism. Choose $f\in \mathcal{A}(U)$ and divide $U$ into its connected components $U = \sqcup V_i $ so that $f(V_i) = a_i \in \mathcal{A}$. By the commutativity of the diagram of the definition of sheafification, then $b_i = \varphi _{V_i}(a_i)$, giving $b \in \mathcal{G}(U)$. The map $\psi : \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{G}$ such that $\varphi _U(f) = b$ is well defined and accomplishes the universal property.
\end{proof}

\begin{problem}
\textbf{2}.
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item For any morphism of sheaves $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} $, show that for each point $P$, $(\ker \varphi )_P = \ker (\varphi )_P$  and $(\im \varphi )_P = \im(\varphi )_P$ .
    \item Show that $\varphi $ is injective (resp. surjective) if and only if the induced map on the stalks $\varphi _P$ is injective (resp. surjective) for all $P$.
    \item Show that a sequence $\cdots \mathcal{F}^{i-1} \xrightarrow{\varphi ^{i-1}} \mathcal{F}^i  \xrightarrow{\varphi ^i} \mathcal{F}^{i+1} \cdots   $ of sheaves and morphisms is exact if and only if for  each $P \in X$ the corresponding sequence of stalks is exact as a sequence of abelian groups. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}

\begin{proof}
(1) First notice that the morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}  $ induces a morphism in stalk $\varphi _P : \mathcal{F}_P \to \mathcal{G}_P  $  

We prove $\ker (\varphi )_{P} \subseteq (\ker \varphi )_P$. Let $<s, U> \in \ker (\varphi )_P$, then clearly $s|_U \in  \ker (\varphi )$ and we have by the induce morphism $\varphi (s|_U)= 0 \implies \varphi _P(<s,U>)=0 \implies <s,U> \in \ker (\varphi _P)$.

We now prove the other inclusion $(\ker \varphi )_P \subseteq \ker (\varphi _P)$. Let $s_P \in \ker (\varphi _P)$, then there exists an open set $U$ and a section $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ such that $s_P = <s,U>$. Thus, $\varphi (s)|_P = 0$ and there exists $V \subseteq U$ such that $\varphi (s)|V = 0$. Hence, $s|_V \in \ker (\varphi )$ and finally $<s,U>=<s,V> \in \ker (\varphi_P )$.


(2) We must prove that $\ker (\varphi )=0 \iff \ker (\varphi _P) = 0 \ \forall  P$ for the injective case. This is a direct consequence of the part (1). In the case of the surjective case, this follows from the fact that $\im \varphi = \mathcal{G} \iff \im (\varphi _P) = \mathcal{G}_P$, which is also seen in the part (1). 

(3) The sequence is exact if and only if $\ker (\varphi ^{i}) = \im (\varphi ^{i-1})$  and we have
\[
\ker (\varphi ^i) = \im (\varphi ^{i-1}) \iff \ker (\varphi ^i)_P = \im(\varphi ^{i-1}) \ \forall  P \iff \ker (\varphi ^i_P) = \im (\varphi ^{i-1}_P) \ \forall  P
\]
the last condition meaning that the stalk sequence is, indeed, an exact sequence.
\end{proof}

\begin{problem}
\textbf{3}. 
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ be a morphism of sheaves on $X$. Show that$\varphi $ is surjective if and only if the following condition holds: for every open set $U\subseteq X$, and for every $s\in \mathcal{G}(U)$, there is a covering $\{U_i\}$ of $U$, and there are elements $t_i \in \mathcal{F} (U_i) $, such that $\varphi (t_i) = s|_{U_i}$ for all $i$.
    \item Give an example of a surjective morphism of sheaves $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} $, and an open set $U$ such that $\varphi (U): \mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{G}(U)$ is not surjective. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}

Following the exercise (2) we have to prove that
\[
\varphi _P \text{ surjective} \ \forall   P \iff \ \forall \begin{cases}
U \subseteq X\\
s \in \mathcal{G} (U) 
\end{cases}  \quad \ \exists \begin{cases}
(U_i) \text{ open cover of } U \\
t_i \in \mathcal{F} (U_i)
\end{cases} : \varphi (t_i) = s |_{U_i}
\]
First we prove $\boxed{ \implies } $. Since $\varphi _P$ is surjective, then $\ \forall t \in \mathcal{G}(V)$ we have a $s \in \mathcal{F}(U) $ such that $<t, V > = <\varphi (s), U> \in \mathcal{G}_P$. In particular, we can restrict more both open sets to an open set $W \subseteq  U \cap V$ defined to be the one such that $s|_W = \varphi (t|_W)$. Thus, we get an open cover $(W_P)_{P \in X}$ of $X$ and sections $t_{W_P} \in \mathcal{F}(W_P) $ satisfying the condition. 

We now prove $\boxed{ \impliedby } $. Given a covering $(U_i)$ and the sections $t_i \in \mathcal{F}(U_i)$ such that $\varphi (t_i) = s|U_i$. Then, given an element of the stalk $<s, U> \in \mathcal{G}_P $, for all point $P$ we have a set $U_i$, and $\varphi _P(<t,U_i>) = <s,U>$, concluding that $\varphi _P$ is surjective.

(2) Consider the following classical example. Let $X = \mathbb{C}^* = \mathbb{C}-\{0\}  $ $\varphi : \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{O}$, with $\mathcal{O}(U) $ the additive group of holomorphic functions on $U$, $\mathcal{O}^*(U)$ the multiplicative group of holomorphic functions on $U$, and $\varphi (f) = e^{2\pi if}$ the exponential mapping (that is a morphism of sheaves). 

It is clear that $z \in \mathcal{O}(\mathbb{C}^*)$ is not the image of any function of $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{C} )$, since the logarithm is not holomorphic in $ \mathbb{C}^*$. But the morphisms induced at the stalks are clearly surjective, so the sheaves morphism is surjective.


\begin{problem}
\textbf{5}. Show that a morphism of sheaves is an isomorphism if and only if it is both injective and surjective.
\end{problem}

Again, we take advantage of the fact that $\varphi :\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}  $ is injective (resp. surjective) if and only if the induce morphisms on stalks $\varphi _P$ are injective (resp. surjective) for all $P$ . We also recall, by proposition 1.1 of \cite{Har}, that $\varphi$ is an isomorphism if and only if $\varphi _P$ is an isomorphism for all $P$. Thus, we have
\[
\varphi \text{ inj, sur } \iff \varphi _P \text{ inj, surj } \ \forall  P \iff  \varphi _P \text{ iso } \iff \varphi \text{ iso }
\]


\begin{problem}
\textbf{6}.
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item Let $\mathcal{F} $ a subsheaf of $\mathcal{F} $. Show that the natural map of $\mathcal{F} $ to the quotient sheaf $\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{F'}  $ is surjective and has kernel $\mathcal{F}' $. Thus there is an exact sequence
    \[
    0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F} / \mathcal{F}' \to 0 
    \]
    \item Conversely, if $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \xrightarrow{\varphi }   \mathcal{F} \xrightarrow{\psi }   \mathcal{F}' \to 0 $ is an exact sequence, show that $\mathcal{F}' $ is isomorphic to a subsheaf of $\mathcal{F} $, and that $\mathcal{F} ''$ is isomorphic to the quotient of $\mathcal{F} $ by this subsheaf.
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}

\begin{proof}
(1) Since $\mathcal{F}_p \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}_p / \mathcal{F}'_p = (\mathcal{F} / \mathcal{F}')_p$ is clearly surjective, with kernel $\mathcal{F}'_p $ , then $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F} / \mathcal{F}' $ is surjective with kernel $ \bigcup \mathcal{F}'_p = \mathcal{F}'  $.

(2) We have
\[
\begin{cases}
 \ker (\psi ) = \im(\varphi ) \cong \mathcal{F}'  \\
 \mathcal{F}''  \cong \im(\psi ) \cong \mathcal{F}/ \ker (\psi )  
\end{cases} \implies \mathcal{F}'' \cong \mathcal{F} / \mathcal{F'}
\]
\end{proof}


\begin{problem}
\textbf{7}. Let $\varphi :\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} $ a morphism of sheaves.
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item Show that $ \im(\varphi ) \cong \mathcal{F} / \ker (\varphi ) $ 
    \item Show that $ \coker (\varphi ) \cong \mathcal{G} / \im(\varphi )$
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}

\begin{proof}
(1) We have the isomorphisms on the stalks
\[
\im (\varphi )_P = \im (\varphi _P) \cong \frac{\mathcal{F}_P}{\ker (\varphi _P)} = \frac{\mathcal{F}_P }{\ker (\varphi )_P} = \left( \frac{\mathcal{F} }{\ker (\varphi )} \right)_P \ \forall  P \implies \im(\varphi ) = \frac{\mathcal{F}}{\ker (\varphi )}
\]

(2) We have the isomorphisms on the stalks
\[
   \coker (\varphi )_P = \coker (\varphi _P)  \cong \frac{\mathcal{G} _P}{ \im (\varphi _P)} = \frac{\mathcal{G}_P }{ \im (\varphi )_P} = \left( \frac{\mathcal{G} }{ \im (\varphi )}  \right)_P \ \forall  P \implies  \coker (\varphi ) \cong \frac{\mathcal{G} }{ \im(\varphi )}
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{problem}
\textbf{8}. For any open subset $U \subseteq X$, show that the functor $\Gamma (U, \cdot )$ from sheaves on $X$ to abelian groups is a left exact functor.

\begin{proof}

\end{enumerate}

\begin{proof}
(1) Let $\varphi , \psi \in \Hom (\mathcal{F}|_U , \mathcal{G}|_U)$ and define the sum of two elements $\varphi , \psi $ as $(\varphi +\psi )(s) \coloneq \varphi (s) + \psi (s)$. Then $\Hom (\mathcal{F}|_U, \mathcal{G}\_U  )$ is clearly abelian.

\end{proof}

\begin{problem}
\textbf{17}. Let $X$ be a topological space, let $P$ be a point, and let $A$  be an abelian group. Define a sheaf $i_p(A)$ on $X$ as follows: $i_P(A)(U) = A$ if $P \in U, 0 $ otherwise. Verify that the stalk of $i_P(A)$ is $A$ at every point $Q \in \overline{P}$. Show that this sheaf could also be described as $i_* (A)$, where $A$ denotes the constant sheaf $A$ on the closed subspace $\overline{P}$, and $i: \overline{P} \to X$  is the inclusion.
\end{problem}

\begin{proof}
We denote $\overline{P} \coloneq \{P\}^-$. If $Q \in \overline{P}$, that means that for every open set $V \ni Q$ we have $P \in V$, and by definition of the sheaf $i_P(A)(V) = A$. The stalk is the limit of a constant group $A$, thus $i:P(A)_Q = A$. Conversely if $Q \in \overline{P}$ then there exists an open set $V \not\ni P$, so the direct limit is $i_P(A)_Q = 0$.

We have that $i_*(A)(U) = A(i^{-1}(U))$. If $P \in U$, then $i^{-1}(U) = \overline{P}$, and if $P \not\in U$, then $i^{-1}(U) = \emptyset$. Hence
\[
\begin{cases}
A (\overline{P}) = A &\text{ if } P \in U\\
A(\emptyset ) = 0 &\text{ if } P \not \in U
\end{cases}
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Schemes}

A scheme is a construction that generalizes in some sense the notion of algebraic variety. Usually, when introducing schemes, the first step is the study of \textit{affine schemes}. Schemes in general are topological spaces that are locally affine schemes. As an analogy we can think of a manifold as a scheme, and its charts locally homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ as the local affine schemes of the scheme. Thus, if the global study of a manifold is the study of how are the charts glued, working with schemes will be the study of how are affine schemes glued.

We will work, thus, for the rest of the chapter with a ring $R$ and the topological space $\Spec(R)$ with the Zariski topology defined as follows

\begin{definition}[Zariski topology] Given $S \subseteq R$, we define the associated closed set in the Zariski topology of $\Spec(R)$ as
    \[
    V(S) = \{[\mathfrak{p} ] \in \Spec(R) : S \subseteq \mathfrak{p}  \}
    \] 
\end{definition}

Note the similarity with the algebraic variety. In some sense we require for the closed set of the topological space to be the ideal that contains certain elements of $R$. We can think now, for instance, in $R = \mathbb{C} [x,y]$. The spectrum $\Spec{R}$ will contain elements of three different forms: $(0), (x-a,y-b)$ and $(f(x,y))$ with $f$ irreducible. In an algebraic variety we had
\[
V(f_1, \dots, f_k) = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{C}^2: f_i(x,y) = 0 \ \forall  i = 1,\dots,k \}
\]

With the definition of closed set associated to a $S \subseteq R$ we have the same idea, now the polynomial functions $f_i$ extend to elements of an abstract ring $R$, but they keep acting somewhat like functions, and the points of the affine space extend to the elements of the spectrum $\Spec(R)$. 

It can be seen that we can construct a basis of the Zariski topology on $\Spec(R)$ with the open sets
\[
X_f \coloneq  \Spec(R) - V(f)
\]

This suggests that the resulting space is almost never Hausdorff, since the open sets are too large and cannot be separated.

\begin{proposition} There is an identification between $X_f$ and the localization of the spectrum of $R$ at one element of the ring
    \begin{align*}
    X _f &\leftrightarrow \Spec (R_f)\\
    \mathfrak{p} &\mapsto \mathfrak{p} R_f
    \end{align*}
\end{proposition}

We now have gathered all the necessary information to define properly what is an affine scheme.

\begin{definition}[Affine scheme] Let $R$ be a ring. An affine scheme is a pair $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ where $X = \Spec(R)$ satisfying
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=-6pt, itemsep=0pt]
    \item $X$ is a topological space with the Zarisky topology, with a basis of open sets
    \[
    X_f \coloneq \{\mathfrak{p} \in \Spec (R) : f \not\in \mathfrak{p}  \} \text{ with } f \in R
    \]
    \item $\mathcal{O}_X$ is a sheaf of rings called the structure sheaf such that $\mathcal{O}_X(X_f) = R_f$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

One should check that the sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X $ is, indeed, well defined. Given $X_g \subseteq X_f$, the restriction map $\rho_{X_f, X_g}$  will have the form $R_f \to R_g$, but this is in fact an inclusion, since $R_g = R_{fg}$, as some power of $g$ is a multiple of $f$. It can also be shown that glueing and locality axiom hold.

Following the example in which $R = \mathbb{C}[x,y]$, suppose that we have the element $f = y-x^2 \in \mathbb{C}[x,y]$. Then the associated closed set is clearly $V(f) = (y-x^2) \in \Spec(R)$, since $y-x^2$ is irreducible. This case is totally analogous to the classical algebraic variety, in which the closed subset will be $V(f) = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{C}^2: y-x^2 = 0 \}$.

Another classical example is the study of the ring $R = \mathbb{Z}$. Now the spectrum will be formed by the ideal $(0)$ and the ideals $(p)$ with $p$ prime number. Thus, we can perform an explicit description of the shape of the open sets in the basis. If $a \in \mathbb{R}$ is decomposable in the primes $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ (with certain multiplicity), then 
\[
V(a) = \{[\mathfrak{p} ]\in \Spec(\mathbb{Z}) : a \subseteq \mathfrak{p} \} = \{(p_1), \ldots , (p_k)\}
\]
and the open set $X_a$ will be the complement.

Attending to the picture, we can see that, if we consider the intersection of two arbitrary open sets (say, $X_6$ and $X_{10}$), the sheaf of the union is perfectly well defined as it should be
\[
    \begin{cases}
&\mathcal{F}(X_6)  = \{ \frac{a}{2^n3^n} : n \ge 0, a \in \mathbb{Z}\}\\
&\mathcal{F}(X_{10})  = \{ \frac{a}{2^n5^n} : n \ge 0, a \in \mathbb{Z}\} 
    \end{cases} \quad  \implies \quad  \mathcal{F}(X_6 \cup X_{10}) = \mathcal{F}(X_2)  = \{ \frac{a}{2^n} : n \ge 0, a \in \mathbb{Z}\}
\]

As a final comment, notice that the stalk of the structure sheaf at a point $[\mathfrak{p} ]\in \Spec(R)$ is, precisely, the ring localized at the prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$. This is a local ring, hence the name of \textit{localization}.
